Overview

In this assignment we built a physically-based path tracer from the ground up. Starting from a blank renderer, we incrementally added every major component of the rendering pipeline: camera ray generation, ray–primitive intersection, a bounding volume hierarchy (BVH) for acceleration, direct and indirect illumination via Monte Carlo integration, and finally adaptive sampling to allocate more samples where the image needs them most.

The end result is a renderer capable of producing photorealistic images with soft shadows, color bleeding, and global illumination effects that rasterization-based pipelines fundamentally cannot replicate. Perhaps the most striking insight from building this is how much physical realism follows naturally from one deceptively simple rule: trace a ray, bounce it off surfaces according to their material properties, and average the results. The complexity lies entirely in making that process efficient enough to be practical — which is where the BVH and adaptive sampling come in.

We also found it illuminating (no pun intended) to see the contribution of each individual bounce of light in isolation. The second and third bounces in particular reveal the indirect color bleeding and soft fill light that make rendered scenes look natural rather than artificially flat — something that is impossible to fake cheaply with rasterization.

Part 1: Ray Generation and Scene Intersection

Ray Generation and the Rendering Pipeline

The rendering pipeline begins at the pixel. For each pixel in the output image, we generate one or more camera rays and average their estimated radiance to produce the final color. Concretely, PathTracer::raytrace_pixel(x, y) samples ns_aa random 2D offsets within the pixel using a uniform grid sampler, normalizes each sample to [0,1]² image coordinates, and calls Camera::generate_ray() to produce a world-space ray for each sample. The per-ray radiance estimates are averaged into a Monte Carlo estimate of the pixel's true color.

Camera::generate_ray(x, y) maps normalized image coordinates to a ray in three steps. First, the 2D image coordinate is mapped to a point on the virtual camera sensor, which sits on the z = −1 plane in camera space. The sensor spans [−tan(hFov/2), tan(hFov/2)] horizontally and [−tan(vFov/2), tan(vFov/2)] vertically, so the mapping is: 

cam_x = (2x − 1) · tan(hFov / 2)
cam_y = (2y − 1) · tan(vFov / 2)

This places (0,0) at the sensor's bottom-left and (1,1) at its top-right, consistent with the convention that normalized image coordinates have their origin at the bottom-left. The camera-space ray direction is then (cam_x, cam_y, −1), normalized. Finally, the direction is rotated into world space using the camera-to-world matrix c2w, and the ray's origin is set to the camera position pos. The ray's min_t and max_t are initialized to nClip and fClip respectively, so only geometry within the viewing frustum is considered.

Once a ray is in the scene, it is tested against every primitive (triangles and spheres) to find the closest intersection. The intersection records the hit distance t, the surface normal, the material (BSDF), and a pointer to the primitive. This information is then used by the lighting routines in later parts to shade the point.

Triangle Intersection: Möller–Trumbore Algorithm

We implemented ray–triangle intersection using the Möller–Trumbore algorithm, which solves for the intersection parameter t and the barycentric coordinates (u, v) simultaneously without explicitly computing the plane equation of the triangle.

Given triangle vertices p1, p2, p3 and a ray with origin o and direction d, we define the edge vectors and a helper vector: 

e1 = p2 − p1
e2 = p3 − p1
s  = o  − p1
s1 = d  × e2
s2 = s  × e1

The algorithm then solves the system in closed form: 

[ t ]       1          [ dot(s2, e2) ]
[ u ] = ———————————  · [ dot(s1, s)  ]
[ v ]   dot(s1, e1)    [ dot(s2, d)  ]

The third barycentric coordinate is recovered as w = 1 − u − v. An intersection is valid when all of the following hold:

  • r.min_t ≤ t ≤ r.max_t (within the ray's active range)
  • u ≥ 0, v ≥ 0, w ≥ 0 (point lies inside the triangle)
  • dot(s1, e1) ≠ 0 (ray is not parallel to the triangle plane)

When a valid intersection is found in Triangle::intersect(), we update r.max_t = t so subsequent intersection tests automatically discard farther hits. The surface normal at the intersection is computed by interpolating the three vertex normals using the barycentric coordinates: 

n = u·n1 + v·n2 + w·n3

This gives smooth per-pixel shading even on coarse meshes.

Sphere Intersection

Ray–sphere intersection reduces to solving a quadratic equation. For a sphere centered at o with radius r and a ray p(t) = ray.o + t·ray.d, substituting into the sphere equation yields: 

a = dot(d, d)
b = −2 · dot(o − ray.o, d)
c = dot(o − ray.o, o − ray.o) − r²

discriminant = b² − 4ac

If the discriminant is negative there is no intersection. Otherwise the two roots give two candidate t values; we pick the smaller one that still falls within [min_t, max_t], falling back to the larger root if the smaller one is out of range. The surface normal at the hit point is simply the normalized vector from the sphere center to the intersection point, which is computed analytically via the built-in normal() helper.

Part 2: Bounding Volume Hierarchy

BVH Construction Algorithm

We construct the BVH recursively. At each call, we first compute the bounding box that encloses all primitives in the current range and initialize a new BVHNode with it. If the number of primitives is at or below max_leaf_size, we mark the node as a leaf by setting its start and end iterators and return immediately.

For interior nodes, we choose the axis to split along by looking at the extent of the bounding box — the axis with the largest range (bbox.extent) is selected. This ensures we always split along the dimension where primitives are most spread out, which tends to produce more balanced subtrees and better ray culling than splitting along a fixed axis.

Splitting Heuristic: Surface Area Heuristic (SAH)

Rather than simply splitting at the centroid midpoint, we implemented the Surface Area Heuristic (SAH) using 8 uniform buckets along the chosen axis. For each primitive, we compute which bucket its centroid falls into and accumulate per-bucket bounding boxes and counts. We then sweep left and right across bucket boundaries to compute prefix and suffix bounding boxes and counts (lboxes, rboxes, lcounts, rcounts).

For each of the 9 candidate split positions (before bucket 0, between each pair of buckets, and after bucket 7), we evaluate the SAH cost: 

cost = C_trav + SA(left) × N(left) + SA(right) × N(right)

where SA() is the surface area of a bounding box, N() is the primitive count on that side, and C_trav = 1.2 is a fixed traversal cost. We pick the split with the lowest cost. This heuristic approximates the expected number of ray–primitive intersection tests, so it naturally prefers splits that produce small, tight child bounding boxes with few primitives.

After determining the best split bucket, we use std::partition to reorder the primitive iterator range so that all primitives whose centroid falls in buckets 0 through best_split − 1 come before those in buckets best_split through 7. As a fallback, if the partition produces an empty left or right side (which would cause infinite recursion), we fall back to a median split using std::nth_element along the same axis, guaranteeing both children always receive at least one primitive.

Bounding Box Intersection

BBox::intersect() uses the slab method. For each axis we compute the entry and exit t values where the ray crosses the two axis-aligned planes bounding the box on that axis, taking care to swap them if the ray direction is negative. The overall entry time is the maximum of the three per-axis entry times, and the overall exit time is the minimum of the three per-axis exit times. If entry exceeds exit, or the exit is before r.min_t, or the entry is after r.max_t, there is no valid intersection. Otherwise we update t0 and t1 with the entry and exit times.

BVH Traversal

Both BVHAccel::has_intersection() and BVHAccel::intersect() follow the same recursive pattern. We first test the ray against the current node's bounding box — if it misses, we return immediately without visiting any children. If the node is a leaf, we test the ray against every primitive it contains. For interior nodes, we recurse into both children. In has_intersection() we short-circuit and return true as soon as any primitive is hit. In intersect() we must visit both children to guarantee we find the closest hit, relying on the fact that each primitive's intersect() updates r.max_t to the nearest hit so far, which automatically prunes farther intersections in subsequent tests.

Part 3: Direct Illumination

Diffuse BSDF

For a Lambertian (diffuse) surface, light is reflected equally in all directions across the hemisphere regardless of the incoming or outgoing direction. The BSDF is therefore simply the reflectance divided by π, ensuring energy conservation when integrated over the hemisphere: 

f(wo, wi) = reflectance / PI

Zero-Bounce Illumination

zero_bounce_radiance() handles the case where the camera ray hits a light source directly without any bouncing. It simply returns the emission of the intersected surface's BSDF via bsdf->get_emission(). This ensures that area lights themselves appear bright white in the rendered image.

Direct Lighting: Uniform Hemisphere Sampling

estimate_direct_lighting_hemisphere() estimates the direct lighting at a hit point by uniformly sampling num_samples directions over the hemisphere centered at the surface normal. For each sampled direction w_j (in object space), we construct a shadow ray from hit_p in that world-space direction with min_t = EPS_F to avoid self-intersection. If that ray hits any surface in the scene, we check whether that surface is emissive. If it is, we accumulate its contribution using the Monte Carlo estimator of the reflection equation: 

L_out += L_emission * f(w_out, w_j) * cos(theta) / pdf

where cos(theta) = w_j.z in the local object frame (since the normal aligns with Z), and pdf = 1 / (2π) for uniform hemisphere sampling. The final result is divided by num_samples to average the estimates. Because we sample directions blindly without targeting lights, most samples miss all light sources entirely, leading to high variance and noisy renders — especially with few samples.

Direct Lighting: Importance Sampling Lights

estimate_direct_lighting_importance() addresses the noise problem by sampling directions directly toward each light source rather than uniformly over the hemisphere. For each light in the scene, we call light->sample_L() which returns the emitted radiance, a world-space direction w_j toward the light, the distance to the light distToLight, and the PDF of that sample. We then cast a shadow ray from hit_p in direction w_j with min_t = EPS_F and max_t = distToLight — if nothing blocks this ray before the light, the light contributes to the hit point. The contribution is accumulated as: 

L += L_j * f(w_out, w_j) * cos(theta) / pdf

For delta lights (point lights), we only take one sample since all samples from a point light are identical. For area lights we take num_lights × ns_area_light samples and average them. Since every sample is guaranteed to point toward a light source, variance drops dramatically compared to hemisphere sampling, producing much cleaner soft shadows with the same sample budget.

Hemisphere vs. Importance Sampling: Analysis

Uniform hemisphere sampling and importance sampling both estimate the same direct lighting integral, but they differ drastically in efficiency. With hemisphere sampling, rays are cast in random directions across the full hemisphere — the vast majority of these never hit a light source and contribute nothing to the estimate, resulting in high variance and visibly grainy renders even at 64 samples per pixel. Importance sampling eliminates this wasted effort by sampling directions directly toward each light source, guaranteeing that every sample carries useful information. This concentrates samples where they matter most, reducing variance dramatically and producing much cleaner soft shadows at the same sample budget. The tradeoff is that importance sampling requires knowledge of where lights are in the scene, whereas hemisphere sampling is more general — but for direct illumination with explicit lights, importance sampling is strictly superior in practice.

Part 4: Global Illumination

Implementation of the Indirect Lighting Function

Global illumination extends direct lighting by accounting for light that bounces multiple times through the scene before reaching the camera. The core of this is at_least_one_bounce_radiance(), which recursively estimates the contribution of indirect light at each hit point.

When called, the function first computes the one-bounce (direct) radiance at the current hit point via one_bounce_radiance(). It then samples a new incoming direction w_in using bsdf->sample_f(), which draws from a cosine-weighted hemisphere distribution and returns both the sampled direction and its PDF. A new ray is constructed from the hit point in the world-space direction corresponding to w_in, with min_t = EPS_F to avoid self-intersection, and its depth is incremented by 1.

If this new ray intersects the scene, we recursively call at_least_one_bounce_radiance() on the new hit point and accumulate its contribution weighted by the BSDF value, the cosine term |w_in.z|, and divided by the PDF: 

L_out += at_least_one_bounce_radiance(new_ray, new_isect)
         * bsdf->f(w_out, w_in) * fabs(w_in.z) / pdf

Recursion terminates when r.depth >= max_ray_depth, at which point the function returns black. When isAccumBounces is set to false, we zero out L_out before adding the recursive contribution so that only the light from the exact max_ray_depth-th bounce is returned, rather than the accumulated sum of all bounces up to that depth. This allows us to visualize the isolated contribution of each individual bounce.

Russian Roulette termination is implemented by randomly terminating paths with a fixed probability, making the estimator unbiased while allowing arbitrarily deep paths. The contribution of surviving paths is scaled up by dividing by the continuation probability to compensate for the terminated ones.

Part 5: Adaptive Sampling

What is Adaptive Sampling?

Uniform sampling allocates the same number of rays to every pixel regardless of how difficult that pixel is to render. Simple regions — like flat walls or areas of uniform color — converge quickly and do not need many samples, while complex regions — like shadow boundaries, edges of objects, or areas with high frequency lighting variation — require many more samples to reduce noise to an acceptable level. Adaptive sampling exploits this by dynamically allocating more samples to pixels that have not yet converged, and stopping early for pixels that have. This gives a much better quality-per-sample tradeoff compared to uniform sampling at the same maximum sample budget.

Implementation

Adaptive sampling is implemented directly in PathTracer::raytrace_pixel(). Rather than always taking exactly ns_aa samples, we maintain a running loop that checks for convergence every samplesPerBatch samples. For each sample, we compute its illuminance x_k = radiance.illum() and accumulate two running statistics: 

s1 += x_k        // sum of illuminances
s2 += x_k * x_k  // sum of squared illuminances

After every samplesPerBatch samples, we compute the mean and variance of all samples taken so far: 

mu    = s1 / n
sigma = sqrt((s2 - s1*s1/n) / (n - 1))

We then compute the 95% confidence interval half-width: 

I = 1.96 * sigma / sqrt(n)

If I <= maxTolerance * mu, the pixel is considered converged and we stop sampling. Otherwise we continue until we reach the maximum ns_aa samples. The actual number of samples taken is stored in sampleCountBuffer, which is used to generate the sample rate visualization image. The convergence check is only performed every samplesPerBatch samples (default 32) rather than after every single sample, which avoids the overhead of checking too frequently.

The intuition behind the convergence criterion is straightforward: I is small only when the variance of the samples is low or when we have taken enough samples that the estimate is stable. By requiring I <= maxTolerance * mu, we ensure that the confidence interval is tight relative to the mean — i.e., we are 95% confident that the true pixel value lies within a narrow band around our current estimate.
