Overview

In this assignment, we built a real-time cloth simulator from the ground up using a mass-spring system. The core idea is to represent a piece of cloth as a grid of point masses connected by three types of springs — structural, shearing, and bending — each enforcing different physical constraints that together give the cloth its realistic feel and resistance to deformation.

From there, we implemented Verlet integration to simulate how the cloth moves over time under external forces like gravity, as well as spring correction forces derived from Hooke's law. We also incorporated a position correction step based on the Provot 1995 paper to prevent springs from stretching more than 10% beyond their rest length in a single timestep — a practical constraint that keeps the simulation stable without sacrificing visual realism.

We then handled collisions — first with external objects like a sphere and a plane, and then with the cloth itself. Self-collision was the most algorithmically interesting challenge: a naive O(n²) check of every point mass pair is far too slow for real-time use, so we implemented spatial hashing to bucket point masses into 3D volume cells and only check pairs within the same cell. This brought self-collision detection down to something that runs comfortably within each simulation timestep.

Finally, we wrote GLSL shader programs to render the cloth in real time on the GPU — a sharp contrast to the CPU-bound path tracing we did in Homework 3. We implemented diffuse shading, Blinn-Phong lighting, texture mapping, bump and displacement mapping, and environment-mapped mirror reflections. Each shader required thinking carefully about the vertex and fragment pipeline and how per-vertex data flows through to per-pixel color computations.

One of the most interesting takeaways from this assignment was seeing how much physical realism you can extract from a relatively simple model. Tweaking parameters like the spring constant ks, density, and damping produced dramatically different cloth behaviors — from stiff, barely-moving burlap to loose, flowing silk — all driven by the same underlying equations. Seeing the cloth fold realistically on itself without clipping, purely from physics constraints and spatial hashing, was a particularly satisfying moment.

Part 1: Masses and Springs

Building the Grid

The cloth is represented as a num_width_points × num_height_points grid of point masses stored in row-major order. For each grid position (j, i), we compute its world-space coordinates by evenly spacing points across the cloth's width and height dimensions: 

x = width  / (num_width_points  - 1) * j
y = height / (num_height_points - 1) * i

The third coordinate depends on the cloth's orientation. For a VERTICAL cloth, we vary positions over the XY plane and assign each point mass a small random offset in Z between −1/1000 and 1/1000 to break the perfect flatness and allow the simulation to develop natural folds. For a HORIZONTAL cloth, we vary positions over the XZ plane and fix Y to 1. Each point mass is also checked against the cloth's pinned vector — if its (x, y) grid index appears there, its pinned flag is set to true so it will not move during simulation.

Adding Springs

After all point masses are placed, we make three separate passes over the grid to add springs for each constraint type. In all cases, we use pointer arithmetic into the flat point_masses vector, converting 2D grid indices (row, col) to 1D indices via row * num_width_points + col.

Structural constraints connect each point mass to the one directly to its left and the one directly above it. These springs give the cloth its basic grid structure and resist stretching along the cloth's primary axes.

Shearing constraints connect each point mass to its diagonal upper-left and diagonal upper-right neighbors. These springs resist shear deformation — the kind of distortion that would occur if opposite corners of the cloth were pulled in opposite directions.

Bending constraints connect each point mass to the point mass two steps to its left and two steps above it (skipping the immediate neighbor). These springs are weaker than structural ones (scaled by 0.2 in the force computation) and resist the cloth from bending too sharply out of plane.

Part 2: Simulation via Numerical Integration

Implementation

To simulate the cloth's motion over time, we implemented three steps inside Cloth::simulate() that run on every timestep.

First, we compute the total force acting on each point mass. External forces like gravity are computed as F = ma by summing all external accelerations scaled by the point mass's mass, and applied uniformly to every point mass. Spring correction forces are then added using Hooke's law: 

F_s = ks * (||p_a - p_b|| - rest_length)

The force is applied along the vector between the two point masses — equal and opposite on each end. Bending springs are scaled down by a factor of 0.2 since they should exert weaker forces than structural or shearing springs.

Second, we use Verlet integration to update each point mass's position: 

x_{t+dt} = x_t + (1 - damping/100) * (x_t - x_{t-dt}) + a_t * dt²

The damping term slightly scales down the velocity component (x_t - x_{t-dt}) to simulate energy loss from friction and heat. Pinned point masses are skipped entirely — their positions never change.

Third, we apply the Provot 1995 deformation constraint. For each spring, if its current length exceeds 110% of its rest length, we correct the positions of its two endpoint masses to bring it back within that limit. The correction is split equally between both masses unless one is pinned, in which case the full correction is applied to the free mass only.

Effect of Spring Constant (ks)

The spring constant ks controls how stiff the cloth is. With a very low ks = 500 N/m, the springs are weak and offer little resistance to gravity — the cloth stretches and sags dramatically, forming a deep, wide bowl shape with the middle hanging significantly lower than the pinned corners. With a very high ks = 50000 N/m, the springs are much stiffer and strongly resist deformation — the cloth hangs much more flatly between the two pinned points, with noticeably less sagging and a tighter, more rigid overall shape.

Effect of Density

Density controls the mass of each point mass via mass = width * height * density / num_width_points / num_height_points. With a low density of 1 g/cm², the cloth is very light and the spring forces easily hold it up — the cloth hangs relatively flat and high, similar in appearance to a high ks. With a high density of 100 g/cm², each point mass is much heavier and gravity pulls the cloth down more forcefully than the springs can resist, producing a deeper sag and more pronounced curvature — similar in appearance to a low ks. This makes intuitive sense: increasing density is effectively increasing the gravitational load on the same spring system.

Effect of Damping

Damping simulates energy loss in the system by scaling down the velocity component in Verlet integration each timestep. With damping = 0%, there is no energy dissipation — the cloth swings dramatically past its rest position, developing chaotic folds and oscillating wildly for a very long time, as seen in the screenshot which shows the cloth mid-swing with complex creasing. With a high damping = 1%, energy is lost quickly each timestep and the cloth settles smoothly into its natural rest state without any oscillation. Importantly, the final resting positions are identical — damping only affects how quickly and smoothly the cloth converges to that rest state, not where it ends up.

Part 3: Handling Collisions with Other Objects

Sphere Collisions

To handle collisions with a sphere, we check on every timestep whether a point mass has moved inside the sphere by comparing the distance from the point mass's current position to the sphere's origin against the sphere's radius. If the distance is less than or equal to the radius, the point mass is inside the sphere and needs to be corrected.

The correction works by computing a tangent point — the point on the sphere's surface along the ray from the origin to the point mass's current position: 

tangent = (pm.position - origin).unit() * radius + origin

We then compute the correction vector needed to bring the point mass from its last position to the tangent point, and scale it down by friction: 

pm.position = pm.last_position + (tangent - pm.last_position) * (1 - friction)

This effectively bumps the point mass back up to the surface of the sphere, while friction slows down its sliding motion along the surface.

Plane Collisions

For plane collisions, we detect a crossing by checking whether the point mass has moved from one side of the plane to the other between the last and current timestep. Specifically, we check if both the position relative to the plane and the direction of motion are on the same non-positive side of the plane's normal: 

dot(pm.position - point, normal) <= 0 &&
dot(pm.position - pm.last_position, normal) <= 0

If a crossing is detected, we compute the tangent point by projecting the current position onto the plane and apply a correction from the last position to that tangent point, scaled down by friction. A small SURFACE_OFFSET of 0.0001 keeps the point mass just above the plane's surface to prevent clipping through.

Effect of Spring Constant on Sphere Collision

The spring constant ks has a clear effect on how the cloth drapes over the sphere. With a low ks = 500 N/m, the springs are very weak and the cloth is extremely soft and compliant — it wraps tightly and smoothly around the sphere and falls straight down on all sides, with many fine, thin folds hanging closely together beneath the sphere. With the default ks = 5000 N/m, the cloth drapes naturally, conforming well to the sphere's top surface while producing wider, more evenly spaced folds underneath. With a high ks = 50000 N/m, the cloth is very stiff and resists conforming to the sphere's curvature — it sits more rigidly on top with only a few large, flat folds that extend outward like a tent, barely bending at all beneath the equator of the sphere.

Part 4: Handling Self-Collisions

Implementation

To handle self-collisions efficiently, we implemented spatial hashing — a technique that partitions 3D space into discrete box volumes and groups point masses by which box they occupy. This avoids the O(n²) cost of checking every pair of point masses against each other, bringing self-collision detection down to a fast per-cell lookup at each timestep.

The implementation consists of three functions. First, Cloth::hash_position() maps a point mass's 3D position to a unique float that identifies its 3D box volume. The box dimensions are set to: 

w = 3 * width  / num_width_points
h = 3 * height / num_height_points
t = max(w, h)

The position is truncated to the nearest box by integer division, and a unique hash is computed from the resulting integer coordinates using large prime multipliers to minimize collisions: 

hash = fmod(132953 * x, 944689) + fmod(100001 * y, 944689) + fmod(375233 * z, 944689)
hash = fmod(hash, 10007)

Second, Cloth::build_spatial_map() iterates over all point masses, hashes each one's position, and inserts a pointer to it into the corresponding bucket in the hash map. This map is rebuilt from scratch at every timestep to reflect the cloth's updated positions.

Third, Cloth::self_collide() takes a point mass, looks up all candidates in its hash bucket, and checks each candidate to see if it is within 2 * thickness distance. For each pair that is too close, we compute a correction vector that would push them exactly 2 * thickness apart. The final correction applied to the point mass is the average of all pairwise corrections, scaled down by simulation_steps to keep position updates smooth and stable across substeps. Pinned point masses are skipped entirely.

Density affects how the cloth folds on itself by changing how strongly gravity pulls the cloth down relative to the spring forces holding it together. With a low density of 1 g/cm², the cloth is very light and the springs dominate — the cloth falls slowly, spreads out more, and produces looser, wider folds with less stacking. With a high density of 100 g/cm², the cloth is heavy and gravity overwhelms the springs — the cloth falls quickly, crumples more aggressively, and stacks into tighter, more compressed folds.

Effect of Spring Constant on Self-Collision

The spring constant ks affects the cloth's stiffness during self-collision. With a low ks = 500, the cloth is very soft and flexible — it crumples easily under its own weight and produces many small, irregular folds as it collapses. With a high ks = 50000, the cloth is stiff and resists bending — it falls more rigidly and produces fewer, larger, smoother folds, taking longer to settle and bouncing more before coming to rest.

Part 5: Shaders

What is a Shader Program?

A shader program is a small program that runs directly on the GPU in parallel across many threads simultaneously — one thread per vertex or per fragment. Unlike the CPU-based path tracing we implemented in Homework 3, shaders execute in real time, making them suitable for interactive applications like our cloth simulator.

There are two types of shaders we worked with. A vertex shader runs once per vertex and is responsible for transforming per-vertex geometric properties — position, normal, UV coordinates, and tangent vectors — from model space into world space and ultimately into screen space. It writes its outputs as varying values that are then interpolated across the triangle's surface using barycentric coordinates before being passed to the fragment shader.

A fragment shader runs once per fragment (roughly per pixel) after rasterization. It takes the interpolated per-vertex values from the vertex shader and uses them to compute a final color for that pixel, written into out_color. By combining the geometric information from the vertex shader with light positions, camera position, and textures passed as uniform variables, the fragment shader produces all of the lighting and material effects we see — diffuse shading, specular highlights, texture mapping, bump mapping, and reflections.

Blinn-Phong Shading Model

The Blinn-Phong shading model approximates how light interacts with a surface by decomposing the total illumination into three components:

The ambient component is a constant low-level light that simulates indirect illumination from the environment. It ensures no part of the surface is completely black even if it receives no direct light: 

L_a = k_a * I_a

The diffuse component models light scattered equally in all directions from a rough surface. Its intensity depends on the angle between the surface normal n and the light direction l, following Lambert's cosine law: 

L_d = k_d * (I / r²) * max(0, dot(n, l))

The specular component models the glossy highlight seen on shiny surfaces. Rather than using the reflection vector directly, Blinn-Phong uses the halfway vector h = normalize(v + l) between the view direction and the light direction, which is cheaper to compute and produces a slightly different highlight shape: 

L_s = k_s * (I / r²) * max(0, dot(n, h))^p

The exponent p controls the sharpness of the specular highlight — higher values produce a tighter, shinier highlight. In our implementation we used k_a = 0.9, k_d = 0.6, k_s = 0.4, and p = 256.

Texture Mapping

The texture mapping shader samples a 2D texture at the UV coordinates interpolated across each fragment. In Texture.frag, we use the built-in GLSL texture() function to sample u_texture_1 at the fragment's UV coordinate v_uv and write the result directly to out_color. The UV coordinates are passed from the vertex shader as a varying and automatically interpolated across the triangle's surface by the rasterizer.

Bump Mapping

Bump mapping creates the illusion of surface detail by perturbing the surface normal at each fragment based on a height map texture, without actually modifying the geometry. We compute how the height changes in the U and V directions using finite differences: 

dU = (h(u + 1/w, v) - h(u, v)) * k_h * k_n
dV = (h(u, v + 1/h) - h(u, v)) * k_h * k_n

where h(u, v) samples the red channel of the height map texture, k_h is the height scaling factor, and k_n is the normal scaling factor. The local object-space normal is then n_o = (-dU, -dV, 1), which is transformed into world space using the TBN matrix constructed from the tangent t, bitangent b = normalize(cross(n, t)), and normal n: 

TBN = mat3(t, b, n)
n_d = normalize(TBN * n_o)

This displaced normal is then used in place of the original surface normal in the Blinn-Phong lighting computation, creating the appearance of bumps and grooves without any change to the underlying mesh geometry.

Displacement Mapping

Displacement mapping extends bump mapping by actually modifying vertex positions in addition to perturbing normals. In Displacement.vert, each vertex is displaced along its normal direction by an amount proportional to the height map value at its UV coordinate: 

p' = p + n * h(uv) * k_h

The fragment shader then applies the same normal perturbation as bump mapping to keep the lighting consistent with the new geometry. The result is that displacement mapping produces actual geometric detail that is visible in silhouettes and at grazing angles, unlike bump mapping which only affects shading. However, displacement mapping requires a sufficiently dense mesh to represent the displaced geometry accurately — on a coarse mesh, the displacement appears blocky and jagged.

Bump vs. Displacement: Coarseness Comparison

The images below compare bump and displacement mapping on the sphere at two different mesh resolutions: coarse (-o 16 -a 16) and fine (-o 128 -a 128). At the coarse resolution, bump mapping still produces smooth-looking shading since it only modifies normals and is independent of mesh density. Displacement mapping at the coarse resolution looks blocky and jagged because there are too few vertices to faithfully represent the displaced surface. At the fine resolution, displacement mapping improves dramatically — the surface detail matches the height map much more closely and the silhouette shows actual geometric variation. Bump mapping looks similar at both resolutions since it is purely a shading effect with no dependence on geometry.

Mirror Shader

The mirror shader simulates a perfectly reflective surface using an environment map. For each fragment, we compute the outgoing eye ray direction from the camera position to the fragment's world-space position, then reflect it across the surface normal to get the incoming direction: 

w_o = normalize(u_cam_pos - v_position.xyz)
w_i = reflect(-w_o, normalize(v_normal.xyz))

We then sample the cubemap environment texture in the direction of w_i using the built-in texture(samplerCube, vec3) function. This gives each fragment the color of whatever the environment map shows in the reflected direction, creating the illusion of a mirror surface that reflects the surrounding environment without requiring any additional ray casting.
